  • The shelf-life of a color has collapsed to 6-9 weeks. With 73% of consumer purchase intent triggered before the product is read, color carries the brand promise on the shelf, the unboxing, the social share, and the gifting moment. 78% of brand owners report that the ribbon color decision is now made before the SKU spec, not after. This complexity demands a 7-stage catwalk-to-DC flow that connects Pantone, fashion week, social signal, retail calendar, dye house, color lab, and DC replenishment into a single operating cadence. Brands that run this cadence lock 28% repeat order velocity; brands that run a single-Pantone pick repeat at 14-19%.

4. The 7-Stage Catwalk-to-DC Color Flow

The 7-stage flow is the operational rhythm for moving a color from the fashion-week runway to a stocked DC. Each stage has a defined input, a defined output, a defined owner, and a defined cycle time. The 7 stages are what turn color forecasting from a creative exercise into a logistics discipline.

  1. Stage 1 — Signal intake (Day 1-7): Collect the 11-signal inputs, weight each, and produce a 30-color shortlist. Output: a scored 30-color shortlist with weighted rationale. Cycle time: 7 days. Owner: trend strategist.
  2. Stage 2 — Palette draft (Day 8-14): Translate the 30-color shortlist into a 9-pillar draft palette with hero/supporting/accent assignments. Output: a 9-pillar palette draft with volume share. Cycle time: 7 days. Owner: creative director.
  3. Stage 3 — Brand alignment (Day 15-21): Pressure-test the draft against brand DNA, season hero product, retail calendar, and competitor palette. Output: a final 9-pillar palette with 1-2 alternates per pillar. Cycle time: 7 days. Owner: brand owner + creative director.
  4. Stage 4 — Lab dye trial (Day 22-35): Lab-dye each of the 9 colors plus 9 alternates, measure Delta E against Pantone, and rank by closeness. Output: a Delta E ranked list with achievable tolerance. Cycle time: 14 days. Owner: dye house lab + OEM color manager.
  5. Stage 5 — Virtual color approval (Day 36-42): Render the 9 colors on the planned substrate (satin / grosgrain / organza) at 6 lighting conditions (D65, A, F2, cool white, warm white, retail spot) using spectrophotometer-driven render. Output: a 54-frame virtual color sheet. Cycle time: 7 days. Owner: OEM color lab.
  6. Stage 6 — Sample strike-off (Day 43-56): Produce 50 m strike-offs for each of the 9 colors with the planned print, edge, and finish; ship to brand owner for physical approval. Output: a 9-color strike-off set with approval signatures. Cycle time: 14 days. Owner: OEM production + brand owner.
  7. Stage 7 — DC replenishment plan (Day 57-70): Convert the approved palette into a DC replenishment plan with hero color lead time 14 days, supporting color 21 days, accent 28 days. Output: a 12-month replenishment cadence per pillar. Cycle time: 14 days. Owner: brand owner + OEM planner.

The 7-stage flow is run for every season and refreshed every quarter for the supporting colors. Brands that run the full flow sell through at 96% and repeat at 28%; brands that skip stages 4-6 sell through at 71% and repeat at 19%. The flow is also a documented audit trail for any color dispute, chargeback, or social trend miss — three risks that now carry 4-7% margin exposure per incident.
