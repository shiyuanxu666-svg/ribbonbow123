OEM Ribbon Pre-Production Artwork & Pre-Press Workflow: A Factory's Complete Guide

Published: May 22, 2026 Author: MSD Ribbon Production Team Category: OEM Operations

The single most common cause of delayed OEM ribbon shipments and costly reprints isn't weaving, dyeing, or finishing — it's the artwork approval stage. Buyers and manufacturers alike lose weeks to misaligned expectations around art files, color proofs, and strike-off samples. This guide walks through the complete pre-production artwork and pre-press workflow from submission to production green light, so you can hit your launch dates without surprises.

Why the Artwork Stage Makes or Breaks Your Order

When you place an OEM ribbon order, the factory doesn't immediately start production. First, the pre-press team must translate your artwork into production-ready printing cylinders, plates, or screen masters. This process has multiple checkpoints — each one is an opportunity to catch errors before they become 50,000 meters of expensive mistakes.

A typical pre-production workflow runs 10–20 business days, depending on complexity. Skip the diligence here, and you risk:

  • Wrong PMS/Pantone colors on the final product
  • Blurry or misaligned logos on printed ribbons
  • Incorrect repeat length causing pattern misalignment
  • Missing barcode or regulatory text on packaging ribbons
  • Production held up while corrections are made mid-run

Step 1 — Art File Submission: What to Send and Why

The factory's pre-press team needs vector files, not flattened images. Here's the minimum checklist for artwork submission:

Required File Formats

  • Vector source files — AI, EPS, or CDR format (not converted PDFs unless high-resolution)
  • outlined/exported PDF — with all fonts converted to outlines
  • High-resolution raster files — minimum 300 DPI if any photographic imagery is involved
  • Artwork layout PDF — showing exact repeat length and seam position
  • Pantone (PMS) color codes — specify for each color in the design
  • Swatch references — physical Pantone color books or digital swatches (e.g., from X-Rite or Pantone Connect)

Common Mistakes That Cause Delays

  • Submitting low-resolution JPEGs — these can't be used to make printing cylinders; the factory will ask for vector files, adding days
  • Forgetting seam alignment marks — if the artwork needs to tile seamlessly, the factory needs to know where the repeat starts and ends
  • Using RGB instead of CMYK or Pantone — screen colors don't translate accurately to offset or screen printing; always specify Pantone PMS codes
  • Embedded vs. outlined fonts — if fonts aren't outlined, the factory's design software may substitute different fonts

Step 2 — Artwork Review and Technical Check

Once files arrive, the factory's pre-press team conducts a technical review. They check:

  • Resolution suitability for the intended printing method (rotary screen, flexo, or heat-transfer)
  • Color separation feasibility — can the design be reproduced with the available ink stations?
  • Repeat length accuracy — does the pattern tile correctly across the ribbon width?
  • Text legibility at the final ribbon width (some designs look fine on paper but become illegible at 15mm width)
  • Barcode or QR code dimensions — these must meet minimum scan requirements

The factory should return an artwork approval form (AAF) or technical confirmation sheet within 2–4 business days. Review this carefully before proceeding. Any errors at this stage are far cheaper to fix than errors discovered on strike-off samples.

Step 3 — Color Proofing: Understanding the Process

Color proofing is where buyers most often encounter confusion. Here's how it works:

Digital Color Proof (First Stage)

The factory produces a digital proof — a computer-generated simulation of how the colors will print. This is printed on paper, not on actual ribbon substrate. Digital proofs show color accuracy within ±10 ΔE (Delta E) on average, which may not be close enough for brand-critical colors.

What to check on a digital proof:

  • Color accuracy against your physical Pantone color standard
  • Logo proportions and text legibility
  • Pattern repeat alignment
  • Any unexpected halftone dots or pixelation

Strike-Off / Pre-Production Sample (The Real Test)

A strike-off (also called a pre-production sample or PP sample) is actual ribbon printed using production materials and methods. This is your best representation of the final product. However, expect the strike-off to come in a shorter length (typically 3–10 meters) rather than full production yardage.

Important factors that affect strike-off color accuracy:

  • Substrate differences — satin ribbon absorbs ink differently than polyester or grosgrain; a strike-off on one material won't match exactly on another
  • Color tolerance (ΔE) — the industry standard for "acceptable match" is ΔE ≤ 2.0 for luxury brands and ΔE ≤ 4.0 for standard commercial use; agree on this before approval
  • Lighting conditions — always evaluate ribbon samples under D65 (daylight) lighting; fluorescent lighting will make colors look different
  • Strike-off vs. bulk production variance — note that printing on running production equipment may produce slightly different results than the strike-off; ask about the factory's historical variance rate

Step 4 — Strike-Off Approval Timeline and Communication

Typical timeline from artwork approval to strike-off delivery:

  • Artwork & pre-press preparation: 3–5 business days
  • Cylinder/plate making: 2–4 business days
  • Strike-off production and shipping: 5–10 business days (typically sent by courier, international)
  • Total: 10–19 business days for first strike-off arrival

For buyers needing faster turnaround, many factories offer express pre-press service (24–48 hour pre-press, 3–5 day strike-off) at an additional cost of $200–$600 depending on complexity.

Approval communication protocol:

  • Document all comments and revision requests in writing (email or messaging platform)
  • Provide physical Pantone color references — not just PMS code numbers — when requesting color corrections
  • Set a maximum number of approval rounds (typically 2–3 rounds included in the OEM quotation, additional rounds cost extra)
  • Define approval deadline explicitly — if the buyer doesn't respond within the agreed window, some factories will proceed to production, which becomes the buyer's liability

Step 5 — Green Light to Production

Once strike-off is approved, the factory issues a production work order with a start date and estimated completion. At this stage, you should receive:

  • Confirmed production start and finish dates
  • Quality inspection checkpoints (inline inspection during production)
  • Pre-shipment inspection (PSI) arrangement details
  • Shipping schedule and port/warehouse details

Don't skip the pre-production inspection stage even if you're satisfied with the strike-off. Production conditions (ink viscosity, machine tension, humidity) can cause drift from the approved sample, especially on long runs of 5,000+ meters.

Pre-Press Checklist for OEM Ribbon Buyers

Before you submit your next OEM ribbon artwork, run through this checklist:

  • ✅ Vector files provided in AI, EPS, or CDR format
  • ✅ Fonts converted to outlines
  • ✅ Pantone PMS codes specified for all colors
  • ✅ Physical color swatch reference included (especially for non-standard colors)
  • ✅ Seam/repeat position clearly marked on the layout file
  • ✅ Barcode/QR code at a scannable size for the final ribbon width
  • ✅ Color tolerance (ΔE) standard agreed with factory in writing
  • ✅ Maximum number of proof rounds defined in the PO
  • ✅ Approval deadline set with factory
  • ✅ Pre-production inspection (PPI) arranged and scoped

Conclusion

The pre-production artwork workflow exists to protect both buyers and manufacturers. It catches errors early, aligns expectations on color and quality, and creates a documented approval trail that matters if a dispute arises on the final product. Treat this stage with the same rigor you'd apply to product design itself — the return on investment is measured in shipments that arrive on time, in color, and on spec.
