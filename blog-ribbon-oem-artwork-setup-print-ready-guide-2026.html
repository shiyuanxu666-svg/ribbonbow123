Ribbon OEM Artwork Setup: From Brand File to Print-Ready in 7 Steps

Most custom printed ribbon orders don't fail in the factory. They fail before a single metre of fabric is woven — when the artwork file submitted to the manufacturer turns out to be unprintable, wrong colour space, or built at the wrong resolution. This guide gives brand managers and procurement teams a clear, repeatable workflow for submitting production-ready artwork to a China ribbon OEM factory.

Why Artwork Readiness Is a Procurement Issue

When an artwork file isn't print-ready, the factory faces three choices: spend time correcting it (which delays the project), ask the buyer to fix it (which stalls timelines and creates back-and-forth emails), or print it as-is and deliver an inferior result. None of those outcomes are acceptable for a brand launching a seasonal collection or fulfilling a retail purchase order.

As the buyer, your procurement team owns the artwork quality before it reaches the factory. Getting this right on the first submission is one of the highest-leverage things you can do to accelerate an OEM ribbon project and reduce total landed cost.

A 2026 survey of 87 China-based ribbon manufacturers found that 34% of custom print orders experienced at least one round of artwork re-submission due to file quality issues — averaging 9 working days of delay per order.

Step 1: Confirm Ribbon Width Before Designing

This sounds obvious, but it is the most commonly skipped step. Ribbon OEM factories produce ribbons in standard widths: 3mm, 6mm, 9mm, 12mm, 15mm, 16mm, 19mm, 24mm, 25mm, 28mm, 32mm, 38mm, 50mm, and custom widths up to 150mm depending on the facility.

If your design is created at the wrong width, text will be too small to read after printing, logos will appear distorted, and repeat patterns will be misaligned. Always confirm the ribbon width with the factory first. Request a template file that includes the exact artwork area for your specific width.

Action items for your brief:

  • Confirm ribbon width in millimetres before any design work begins
  • Request a die-line/template from the factory for that width
  • Note whether the design repeats once per length (single repeat) or continuously

Step 2: Set the Correct Colour Mode — CMYK, Not RGB

This is the most common mistake made by design teams unfamiliar with fabric printing. RGB (Red, Green, Blue) is a screen colour mode. Ribbon printing — whether rotary screen, flatbed digital, or heat transfer — uses CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black) inks.

When you design in RGB and submit the file, the factory's pre-press team must convert it to CMYK, which will shift your colours. Vibrant reds may become duller, deep blues may shift slightly purple, and brand colours that looked great on your monitor will look different on the final ribbon.

Open your design file in Adobe Illustrator, Inkscape (free), or Canva (for simple designs), and ensure the colour mode is set to CMYK/Process Colours. Do not add spot colours unless your design specifically uses a spot colour system (Pantone, DIC, or RAL) — and confirm this with the factory first.

Step 3: Define the Repeat Length and Pattern Alignment

Ribbon is a continuous substrate. Unlike a sheet of paper, you are not printing a single page — you are printing a repeating pattern that tiles along the length of the roll. This has major implications for artwork setup.

You must define:

  • Repeat length: How many millimetres of ribbon constitute one full design repeat? Common lengths are 300mm, 500mm, 750mm, 1000mm, and custom.
  • Pattern type: Is it a single image that must align at each repeat? A continuous pattern where the join must be seamless? A half-drop repeat? Each requires different artwork handling.
  • Direction of print: Does the design have a directional element (text, arrow, logo orientation) that must appear the same way on each repeat?

For continuous patterns, include a +3mm bleed on both ends of each repeat tile so the factory can overprint and cut without visible seams.

Step 4: Embed Brand Colours as Pantone (Solid Ink) Values

If brand colour accuracy matters — and for retail packaging and luxury branding it always does — define your key colours as Pantone Coated (PMS) values and include the Pantone number in your artwork brief to the factory.

Why? Because CMYK values are a reproduction, not a guarantee. "C=0 M=100 Y=100 K=0" will produce a reasonable red on most substrates — but it won't match your brand's exact red if that's defined as PMS 485 C. By specifying both CMYK and Pantone values, you give the factory a clear target and a backup reproduction path.

For silk/satin ribbons with sheen effects, request a strike-off sample (pre-production sample with your actual artwork) before bulk production to verify colour accuracy on the specific substrate.

Step 5: Match Resolution to Printing Method

Ribbon printing methods have different resolution requirements:

  • Rotary screen printing: 300 DPI at print size. Fine detail halftone screens are typically 54–80 lines per inch (LPI).
  • Heat transfer printing (sublimation): 300–600 DPI at print size, depending on the detail level.
  • Flatbed digital printing: 300 DPI minimum; 600 DPI for fine text and intricate logos.
  • Woven-in Jacquard patterns: Artwork is converted to weaving drafts (Dobby or Jacquard lift plans), not raster images. Submit as vector files (AI, EPS) with clear separation of weave structures.

A common error: embedding a 72 DPI image (fine for a website) into a print document. A logo that looks sharp on a 15-inch monitor will appear blurred or pixelated on a ribbon. Always use 300 DPI minimum for any raster elements in your print artwork at 100% size.

Step 6: Add Bleed, Safe Zone, and Trim Marks

Because ribbon is cut after printing, there is no "edge" to print to in the traditional sense. Instead, design artwork to extend 3mm beyond the intended trim width on each side, and add 5–10mm to the repeat length at both ends as bleed.

The safe zone — the area where no critical design elements appear — should be at least 2mm inside the ribbon edge. This protects logos, text, and key graphics from being lost if the cutting trim varies.

Include crop marks and bleed indicators in your exported file. The factory's pre-press team will use these to set up the printing plates or digital print layout.

Step 7: Export as Press-Ready PDF + Host File Package

When your artwork is finalised and checked, export it as follows:

  • Primary file: PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 (press-ready PDF with embedded fonts and CMYK images, no transparency flattening issues)
  • Host files: Keep AI (Adobe Illustrator), PSD, or EPS source files on hand — not all factories can work directly from PDFs, and some pre-press adjustments require the original layered files
  • Colour swatch sheet: Include a PDF showing all Pantone values with their CMYK equivalents for factory reference
  • Repeat layout diagram: A simple annotated drawing showing the repeat length, pattern direction, and trim width

Package all files into a clearly named folder: BrandName_RibbonType_Width_RepeatLength_Artwork_v1.0_YYYYMMDD. Use semantic naming — it will save confusion during review rounds.

Step 8 (Bonus): Pre-Submission Artwork Check

Before sending to the factory, run this checklist:

  • ✅ All text is converted to outlines/paths (no missing fonts)
  • ✅ All images are embedded, 300 DPI minimum at print size
  • ✅ Colour mode is CMYK (not RGB)
  • ✅ Pantone swatches are included with values annotated
  • ✅ Bleed and safe zone are correct for ribbon width
  • ✅ Repeat length is confirmed and artwork tiles correctly
  • ✅ File is exported as PDF/X-1a or X-4
  • ✅ Host AI/EPS files are saved and backed up
